Investment Class Miracle

By Stephen M. Bird. Posted June 13, 2015.   Print this Article

During the Winter Quarter at Weber State College in 1979, I enrolled in a course about Investments. The instructor’s tests contained no “multiple choice,” “true or false,” or “matching” style questions. Instead, he asked questions that required us to outline the chapter in detail from memory. He wouldn’t accept any vague responses either. If we, the students, weren’t specific, we received no credit for an answer. To pass his tests, I had to memorize each chapter—point by point. Our textbook contained eleven chapters. We studied one chapter each week and then took a test. This created a special problem for me because I forgot the old chapters as I learned the new ones.

This teacher also created the most difficult final exam I ever took. He said he would number little squares of paper from one to eleven and put them in his hat. Then he said he would draw out three of those squares of paper. We would have to outline in detail the three chapters, which corresponded to the numbers on the three pieces of paper.

I Was In Trouble

One week before the final exam I knew I was in trouble. I had forgotten all of the chapters except the last one, chapter eleven. I couldn’t even remember what each chapter was about. When I checked my schedule for time to review, I saw the soonest available time was the Saturday morning of the final exam. Commitments to my family, church, job and other classes had demanded my time.

I arose that Saturday morning at 5 A.M. to pray. The final exam was at 11:30 A.M., leaving only six hours for study. I said, “Heavenly Father, I don’t know what thou wilt do, but I can only remember chapter eleven (our last exam before the final exam). I should have reviewed, but I didn't. Even though I have little hope, I thought I should approach thee anyway. I know thou hast the power to help me. I know I don’t deserve it, but I ask thee to help me if thou wilt. What should I do? Where should I start my review?”

I received the answer immediately: Start with chapter one and learn it cold. Then do the same with chapter two and so on. Go as fast as you can as far as you can without forgetting anything. That's all you can do. I’ll help you with the rest.

I followed the instructions to the letter, not worrying about anything else. I studied until the last minute before leaving home and arrived at class one minute before the exam started. The instructor seemed to relish our predicament. His face beamed with a smile as he asked for three volunteers. He put his hat upside down on his desk and put in eleven squares of paper, which he had numbered from one to eleven.

My body tensed because I had only been able to study the first two chapters. I could only remember chapters one, two and eleven. I could remember chapter eleven because we'd been tested on it the previous week and I had reviewed it enough to remember its salient points. Anxiously, I awaited the announcement of the first number. If the volunteers drew out any numbers than one, two or eleven, I would fail the test.

The Drawing

The first volunteer drew out a paper and handed it to the teacher who proudly announced, “The first chapter, is chapter number one.”

I let out a deep sigh of relief before the second volunteer trudged to the hat and pulled out chapter two. I was thrilled. I knew these chapters. With rising excitement I waited for the last number. When our teacher received the third piece of paper he said, “The last chapter is eleven.”

Then I thanked Heavenly Father profusely for pulling out the exact three chapters I needed to complete the exam and wrote as fast as I could so I wouldn’t forget anything. Two hours later I submitted my exam grateful it was over.

A few days after the exam, I visited the professor to see how I had done and he said, “You got an ‘A’ on the final exam.”

Some time later, I figured out the statistical probabilities of drawing out those three chapter numbers in the specific order that they had come out of the hat. The mathematical formula is   1/11   x   1/10   x   1/9   =   1/990,   about one chance in a thousand. I don't know what a statistician would think, but it really doesn’t matter to me because I heard my Heavenly Father's voice and I know He made it happen for me.
